INDEX: 7
URL: https://www2.hse.ie/conditions/warning-signs-during-pregnancy/
TITLE: Warning signs during pregnancy
CONTENT:
H1: Warning signs during pregnancy
P: You might get symptoms during your pregnancy that are common and not a cause for alarm.
P: But if you have any symptoms that are causing you concern, contact your midwife, GP or obstetrician immediately. Always trust your instincts. It's best to be safe.
H2: Get urgent help now
H3: Emergency action required: Contact your midwife, GP or obstetrician immediately if:
LI: you are bleeding from the vagina or have severe stomach pain
LI: your baby’s usual pattern of movement has changed
LI: you have a severe headache, especially with other symptoms including blurred vision, upper stomach pain, nausea or swelling
LI: you are very emotionally distressed or feel your mental health is in crisis
LI: one of your calves is red, swollen and hot
LI: you have symptoms like pain when peeing, high temperature, or needing to pee more than normal
LI: any stomach pain feels like tightening
H2: Check common warning signs
P: It can sometimes be hard to tell the difference between a warning sign or a normal pregnancy symptom. Talk to your GP, obstetrician or midwife if you are worried.
P: Common warning signs include:
LI: bleeding from the vagina
LI: stomach pain
LI: shortness of breath or chest pain
LI: swollen ankles, feet and fingers in pregnancy
LI: mental health and emotional distress
LI: a change in your baby's normal movements
H3: Bleeding from the vagina
P: Vaginal bleeding is common during pregnancy. It does not always mean that there is a problem with your pregnancy. But sometimes it can be a dangerous sign.
H3: Important
P: Always speak to your GP, midwife or obstetrician if you notice any bleeding from your vagina.
P: You might bleed for different reasons depending on how far along in your pregnancy you are.
H3: Vaginal bleeding in the first trimester
P: You might have vaginal bleeding during the first trimester (0 to 12 weeks) for any of the following reasons:
LI: implantation bleeding, sometimes called 'spotting' - this happens very early on in your pregnancy (after 6 to 10 days), because the fertilised egg has attached to the inner lining of your womb
LI: hormonal changes - these can cause changes to your cervix (the neck of the womb) that mean you bleed more easily, for example after sex
LI: threatened miscarriage - this is where your pregnancy is developing as normal but you are bleeding from your vagina
LI: miscarriage - sometimes bleeding in pregnancy may mean a loss of a pregnancy
P: In some cases, you might also bleed for the following reasons:
LI: ectopic pregnancy - this is when the pregnancy develops outside the womb
LI: molar pregnancy - this is a rare condition where your pregnancy does not develop normally from the very beginning
H3: Vaginal bleeding in the second and third trimesters
P: Vaginal bleeding during the second trimester (13 to 26 weeks) and third trimester (26 to 40 weeks) is common.
P: But always contact your GP, midwife or obstetrician urgently if you experience any bleeding.
P: You might bleed for any of the following reasons:
LI: 'late' miscarriage - this is the term used to describe the loss of a pregnancy between 12 and 23 weeks
LI: problems with the cervix or neck of the womb - this includes infection or inflammation
LI: placenta praevia - this is where your placenta is lying low in your womb and covering some or all of your cervix (the neck of your womb)
LI: placental abruption (rupture) - this is not common, but is dangerous as the placenta starts to come away, and may be accompanied by tummy pain
P: Vaginal bleeding later on in pregnancy can also be a sign of going into labour, otherwise known as a 'show'. This is especially true if you also have tightenings or pains in your lower tummy.
P: If you are less than 37 weeks pregnant, this might also be a sign of premature labour.
H3: Stomach pain
P: You will normally get some stomach pain while you are pregnant.
P: In your first trimester (weeks 0 to 12) it is common to feel mild pains in your lower tummy area. These are caused by hormonal changes and by your growing womb.
P: You might have more severe stomach pain in your second and third trimester.
P: You might also have stomach pain at any point in your pregnancy because of:
LI: urinary tract infection
LI: constipation or trapped wind
LI: premature or preterm labour - if you are less than 37 weeks pregnant
P: Talk with your healthcare team if you are worried about your stomach pain.
P: Stomach pain and cramps in pregnancy
H3: Change in your baby's movements
H3: Non-urgent advice:  Contact your midwife, obstetrician or maternity hospital immediately if:
P: you notice any change in your baby’s movements
P: A reduction or change in your baby's movements could be a warning sign that:
LI: your baby is unwell
LI: your baby does not have enough fluid around them
LI: there is a risk of you having a stillbirth
P: Your baby's movements during pregnancy
H3: Shortness of breath or chest pain
P: Being short of breath is common during pregnancy, particularly in your third trimester.
H3: Swollen ankles, feet and fingers in pregnancy
P: You might have swollen ankles, feet or fingers at any point in your pregnancy. This is very common.
P: But a sudden increase in your swelling could be a sign of pre-eclampsia. This is a condition that needs to be monitored as soon as possible.
H3: Mental health and emotional distress
P: Mental health issues in pregnancy are common and can be treated.
P: Use the following guides to find out more and get help:
LI: mental health and emotional changes in your first trimester
LI: mental health and emotional changes in your second trimester
LI: mental health and emotional changes in your third trimester
LI: how to help yourself and get help for any mental health concerns
P: Perinatal mental health services are available in all maternity units and hospitals. They provide specialist support if you are experiencing mental health problems in pregnancy. Your GP, midwife, doctor or psychiatrist can refer you to the service.
H3: Other common warning signs
P: Find out more about these other common warning signs during pregnancy:
LI: headaches in pregnancy
LI: varicose veins in pregnancy
LI: leg cramps during pregnancy
P: Page last reviewed: 13 February 2023

TITLE: 0 to 8 weeks pregnant
CONTENT:
H1: 0 to 8 weeks pregnant
P: Your weeks of pregnancy are dated from the first day of your last period. This means that in the first 2 weeks or so, you are not pregnant. Your body prepares for ovulation as usual.
H2: Weeks 1 to 2 - preparing for ovulation
P: You ovulate (release an egg) around 2 weeks after the first day of your period. This depends on the length of your menstrual cycle.
P: All women who can get pregnant are advised to take a 400 micrograms (mcg) folic acid supplement every day. Folic acid supports your baby’s spine and brain development.
P: Folic acid before or during pregnancy
P: What to expect in the first trimester
H2: Week 3 - fertilisation
P: After you release an egg (ovum), it travels down the fallopian tube. This is the tube that transports the egg from the ovary to the womb (uterus).
P: After sex, there may be sperm in the fallopian tube. At the moment of conception, one of the sperm enters and fertilises the egg.
P: The fertilised egg continues to move towards the womb. It begins as a single cell that divides again and again.
P: By the time the fertilised egg reaches the womb, it has become a hollow ball of cells known as a blastocyst. When the blastocyst reaches the womb, it attaches to the lining of the womb and starts to develop into an embryo.
H2: Week 4 - implantation
P: In weeks 4 to 5 of early pregnancy, the blastocyst grows and develops within the lining of the womb.
P: The outer cells reach out and form links with your blood supply. After some time, they will form the placenta (afterbirth).
P: The inner group of cells will develop into the embryo. These inner cells form 3 layers at first. Each of these layers grow to be different parts of the body.
P: These are:
LI: inner layer - this becomes the breathing and digestive systems and includes the lungs, stomach, gut and bladder
LI: middle layer - this becomes the heart, blood vessels, muscles and bones
LI: outer layer - this becomes the brain and nervous system, the eye lenses, tooth enamel, skin and nails
P: In these early weeks, the embryo attaches to a tiny yolk sac. This sac provides nourishment to the embryo.
P: A few weeks later, the placenta is fully formed and takes over the transfer of nutrients to the embryo.
P: Cells from the placenta grow deep into the wall of the womb. Here, they establish a rich blood supply. This makes sure the embryo receives all the oxygen and nutrients it needs.
H2: Week 5 of pregnancy
P: This is the time of the first missed period. It is when most women start to think they may be pregnant.
P: The embryo produces more of the pregnancy hormone (hCG). This causes your ovaries to stop releasing eggs. Your ovaries will also produce more oestrogen and progesterone. These hormones stop your period and help the placenta to grow.
P: The pregnancy hormone, hCG, is also present in your pee. Home pregnancy tests detect the levels of hCG in your pee.
P: Home pregnancy tests
H3: Embryo developing
P: At this stage, the nervous system is developing in the embryo. The foundations for the major organs are also in place. The embryo is around 2mm long and is about the size of an apple seed.
P: The embryo's outer layer of cells form a hollow tube called the neural tube. This will become the brain and spinal cord.
P: At the same time, the heart is forming as a simple tube-like structure. The embryo has some of its own blood vessels and blood begins to circulate. A string of these blood vessels connects you to the embryo, and will become the umbilical cord.
P: If you haven't been taking folic acid, you can still start now.
H4: Confirming your pregnancy with your GP (video)
H2: Week 6 of pregnancy
P: At 6 to 7 weeks, the embryo develops a large bulge where the heart is and a bump at the head end of the neural tube. This bump will become the brain and head. The embryo is curved and has a tail. It looks a bit like a small tadpole.
P: You can sometimes see the heart beating at this stage on a vaginal ultrasound scan.
P: The developing arms and legs become visible as small swellings (limb buds).
P: Little dimples on the side of the head will become the ears, and there are thickenings where the eyes will be. The embryo has a thin layer of see-through skin.
P: By the end of week 6, the embryo is about the size of a pea.
H2: Week 7 of pregnancy
P: By 7 weeks, the embryo has grown to about 10mm long from head to bottom. This measurement is called the 'crown-rump length'.
P: The brain is growing rapidly. This results in the head growing faster than the rest of the body. The embryo has a large forehead, and the eyes and ears continue to develop.
P: The inner ear starts to develop. But the outer ear on the side of the head will not appear for a couple more weeks.
P: The limb buds start to form cartilage, which will develop into the bones of the legs and arms. The arm buds get longer and the ends flatten out. These will become the hands.
P: Nerve cells continue to multiply and develop. The brain and spinal cord (nervous system) starts to form. By the end of week 7, the embryo is about the same size as a grape.

H1: Diagnostic tests during pregnancy

LI: Edwards’ syndrome
LI: Patau’s syndrome

LI: you have had a baby with a serious medical or genetic condition before
H2: Making an informed choice
P: Diagnostic tests are usually the only way to be sure if your baby has certain health problems.
P: Depending on the result, they can:
LI: reassure you that your baby is not at risk of having a specific condition
LI: help you decide if you want to continue with your pregnancy
LI: give you time to prepare for a baby with special needs
LI: help you plan care for your baby after they are born
P: But diagnostic tests can:
LI: be invasive
LI: never be 100% accurate
LI: increase the risk of miscarriage
LI: increase your anxiety - especially if you feel you would continue with your pregnancy regardless of the results
P: Ask your obstetrician or midwife to explain the risks involved in diagnostic tests.
H2: Types of diagnostic tests in pregnancy
P: The most common diagnostic tests done during pregnancy are:
LI: chorionic villus sampling (CVS)
LI: amniocentesis
P: These tests are uncomfortable. It's normal to get cramps like period pain for a few days afterwards.
H3: Chorionic villus sampling (CVS)
P: A CVS is when a small sample of cells from the placenta (afterbirth) is removed. These cells can be used to test your baby for chromosomal and genetic conditions.
P: This is usually done between 11 and 12 weeks of pregnancy, but can sometimes be done up to 14 weeks.
P: There are 2 types of CVS:
LI: a transabdominal CVS - inserting a needle into your tummy
LI: a transcervical CVS - inserting a thin tube into your cervix (the neck of your womb)
P: The type of CVS you have will depend on the position of your placenta. Both types of CVS will be done during an ultrasound scan. This helps the healthcare professional guide the needle or tube into the right place.
H4: Risks of CVS
P: 1 or 2 in every 200 women who have a CVS will have a miscarriage. Symptoms of miscarriage usually start 2 to 3 days after the CVS.
P: 1 to 2 in every 100 women who have a CVS, will have results that may not be a true reflection of their baby’s condition. This is called a false positive. It is when your results wrongly say that your baby has the condition that it was tested for. A false positive happens because of abnormalities in the cells of your placenta, not your baby. This is known as placental mosaicism.
P: Other risks can include infection or the need for a second test if the first does not give a clear result.
H3: Amniocentesis
P: Amniocentesis involves inserting a needle into your tummy during an ultrasound scan. A small sample is taken of the amniotic fluid that surrounds your baby. This can be used to test your baby for chromosomal and genetic conditions.
P: The test is usually done after 15 weeks of pregnancy and is very accurate.
P: The needle does not touch your baby. Your baby’s heartbeat will be checked before and after the amniocentesis.
P: It can take up to 2 to 3 weeks to get the full results.
P: Your obstetrician or specialist midwife will talk to you about how you will get the results.
H4: Risks of amniocentesis
P: 1 in 300 women who have an amniocentesis will have a miscarriage.
P: Other risks include:
LI: infection
LI: bleeding or leaking of amniotic fluid from the vagina
LI: tummy cramping
LI: no clear result - if this happens it will need to be repeated
H3: When to get medical help
P: Both tests are uncomfortable. It's normal to get cramps similar to period pains for a few days afterwards.
H3: Non-urgent advice:  You should contact the maternity hospital immediately if you have:
LI: any fluid leaking from your vagina
LI: severe tummy pain
LI: any bleeding from your vagina
LI: a general feeling of being unwell
LI: a fever or temperature over 38 degrees Celsius

P: Sometimes it can be difficult to find out if your baby has a certain condition. This can mean it will take some time to give you a full diagnosis.

H1: Screening tests during pregnancy
P: During your pregnancy, you'll be offered some routine tests known as screening tests.

P: If you get a ‘high risk' result, you will be referred for more detailed tests called diagnostic tests. A ‘low risk’ result does not mean no risk.
P: Many screening tests are offered to all pregnant women. Other tests are only offered to women who are at high risk of a particular health problem.
P: Screening tests are usually free. But some people choose to pay for non-invasive prenatal screening (NIPS). NIPS is not offered as part of free public maternity services.
H2: Difference between screening tests and diagnostic tests
P: Screening tests are usually non-invasive. This means the tests are done on the outside of your body. There's no risk of them causing a miscarriage.
P: Screening tests include blood tests and ultrasound scans.
P: Diagnostic tests may be more invasive or more expensive than screening tests. They usually tell whether you or your baby has a health problem more precisely. But they can sometimes increase your risk of miscarriage.
P: You will normally only be offered them if you:
LI: are more at risk of a health problem
LI: have had a positive screening test
P: Diagnostic tests include amniocentesis and CVS (chronic villus sampling).
H2: Deciding to have pregnancy screening tests
P: Screening tests can help you make informed choices about:
LI: continuing a pregnancy
LI: care you may need during your pregnancy and the birth
LI: care you or your baby may need after your baby is born
P: They can reduce the risk of you becoming seriously ill during your pregnancy.
P: Choosing whether to have screening tests done is an important choice for you and your baby's health. Only you can make the decision that is right for you, your pregnancy and your baby. Your birth partner or support person will help you make your choice.
H2: Make an informed choice
P: It is important that you have all the information you need to make the right choice for you. Your midwife, obstetrician or GP are there to answer your questions. Ask them about your screening test options. Discuss these with your partner or family and friends.

LI: What is this test for?
LI: What does it mean if the test shows a 'high risk' result?

LI: What could happen if I do not have the test done?

LI: your baby from having a disability or dying

P: Screening tests are not perfect. Sometimes false positive and false negative results can happen.
H3: False positives

P: False positives can be very worrying. They can also mean you or your baby must have more tests.

P: Pregnancy tests detect the levels of a hormone called hCG in your urine (pee). This hormone is produced during pregnancy. It is present in your bloodstream and your urine.
P: In early pregnancy, the amount of the hormone in your system increases. The later you leave it to take a pregnancy test, the higher the hCG levels will be.

LI: had vaginal bleeding
LI: have severe tummy pains
LI: have severe morning sickness

LI: you did the test too early
LI: you did not wait long enough before checking the result (always follow the instructions - results often take 3 minutes to appear)
LI: your urine is very diluted because you drank a lot of water
LI: there is a fault with the test

LI: Mat - this includes getting on all fours on a yoga mat.

LI: fat

H2: Benefits of colostrum
P: Colostrum is sometimes called liquid gold because of its importance and colour. It has a lot of antioxidants and antibodies which can help keep your baby healthy.

P: It's normal to only have a small amount of colostrum. But every drop has great health benefits for your baby for the rest of their life.
H2: Colostrum harvesting while you are pregnant
P: Colostrum harvesting means expressing and collecting your colostrum in the last few weeks of pregnancy (usually from 36 to 37 weeks).
P: Having colostrum ready can be really helpful if your baby needs extra milk after birth.

LI: you have diabetes
LI: you are planning a caesarean birth
LI: you have had breastfeeding challenges before
LI: your baby is expected to need some extra support.

P: Hand expressing in pregnancy is also a useful skill for after your baby is born. For example, if your baby is sleepy at the breast or needs a little encouragement, you can express a few drops to help.

P: The volume of colostrum you will produce varies.

P: If you have breastfed before, your milk may come in sooner and you'll likely have colostrum in larger amounts.
P: If you were collecting colostrum while you were pregnant, the amount you produced is not a guide of your milk supply after your baby is born.

H1: Positioning and attachment (latching on)
P: Positioning and attachment is how you hold your baby at your breast so they can feed. Attachment is also sometimes known as 'latching on'.
P: Correct positioning and attachment is the most important thing for successful breastfeeding. When your baby is well positioned and attached, they will find it easier to feed well.
P: In the early days of breastfeeding, you may feel nipple tenderness or sensitivity as you get used to your baby attaching to your nipple. But it should not be painful.
H3: Ask our breastfeeding experts
P: Get personal advice from a lactation consultant
H2: How to attach your baby
P: There are 2 ways to attach your baby:
LI: baby-led approach
LI: mother-led approach
H2: Baby-led approach
P: The baby-led approach lets your baby use their natural instinct to latch on.
P: How to do it:
LI: Relax in the laid-back position.
LI: Lay your baby tummy-down between your breasts, with easy access to the nipple.
LI: Let them find your breast and attach on their own.
H2: Mother-led approach
P: If the baby-led approach does not work for you, you can take a more active role in helping your baby to attach. This is called the mother-led approach.
H3: Stage 1
P: Hold your baby close to you with their nose level with your nipple.
H3: Stage 2
P: Let your baby’s head tip back so their top lip brushes against your nipple. This helps them open their mouth wide.
H3: Stage 3
P: When your baby’s mouth is wide open, bring them to your breast.
P: Aim your nipple to the roof of their mouth.
P: In the correct position, your baby can suck, swallow and breathe comfortably. If they cannot breathe freely, they come off the breast.

LI: their chin is pressed into your breast
LI: your nipple and most of the areola (the area around your nipple) is deep in your baby’s mouth
LI: more of your areola is visible above their top lip than below their bottom lip
LI: their cheeks appear fuller
LI: their nose is titled back and they can breathe easily
P: When they start feeding, they suck quickly at first. This is followed by longer sucks and you can hear them swallowing.
H2: Breastfeeding positions
P: Before you start breastfeeding, find a comfortable position. Like any new skill, this takes practice. There are very few rules, but it is important that you and your baby are sitting or lying comfortably.
P: Try the following positions to see which works best for you and your baby.
H3: Laid-back position
P: Laid-back is a fantastic breastfeeding position. It lets you relax while your baby is able to attach easily to your breast. This is very important in the early days when you are both getting used to breastfeeding.
P: Laid-back is a great position to try if you are having difficulties with breastfeeding.
P: 5 tips to help you get your technique right:
LI: The movement needed to get into this position is from your pelvis. Shift your hips forward in a chair or bed to create a semi-reclined position. Laying your baby down on your semi-reclined body ensures they connect with your body without any gaps.
LI: Lay your baby on top of you with their tummy down and their cheek resting near your breast.
LI: Allow your baby to self-attach. They will find the breast and attach themselves. You can also guide or help your baby to attach.
LI: You can use your arms as guard rails to support your baby on top of your body.
LI: You can also use pillows and cushions to provide support wherever you need it.

P: Never fall asleep with your baby on a sofa or armchair. This increases the risk of sudden infant death syndrome (cot death).

P: Tips for getting your technique right:
LI: Hold your baby close and facing you. You may find a pillow on your lap helpful to support your baby.

LI: reflux
LI: an ear infection
LI: tongue-tie
LI: low muscle tone
LI: hip dysplasia
LI: oversupply of breast milk

LI: Your baby sits straddling your thigh or on your hip.
LI: Their back and head is upright while breastfeeding.

LI: Support your baby’s neck, back and shoulders with your hand and wrist.
LI: Let your baby’s back lie along your arm. Your baby should be facing you with their nose near your nipple.
LI: Support your breast with your other hand.
LI: Guide your nipple towards the baby's mouth.
LI: Your baby will then snuggle up close and begin to breastfeed.
H3: Side-lying position
P: Side-lying involves lying down. It is good for night feeds
P: Tips for getting your technique right:
LI: You and your baby lie down facing each other.
LI: Make sure your neck and back are supported and comfortable. Your baby's chest should face your chest.
LI: Take the arm that you're lying on and either place it under your head or use it to support your baby under their head or around their body.
LI: You can use a pillow or rolled-up blanket behind your baby's back for support.
LI: Your baby’s nose will be near the nipple and their chin in contact with the underside of the breast.

LI: Your baby should have a wide mouthful of breast in their mouth.
LI: Their chin should be touching your breast.
LI: You may notice their top and bottom lips curled out.
LI: Their cheeks should be full and rounded - you should not see the cheeks dimpling when your baby sucks.
LI: Their jaw should be moving, you may also see their ears twitch as they feed.
LI: They will start with short quick sucks, then change to long deep sucks with pauses to breathe.
LI: You should hear them swallowing as your breast milk volume increases.
LI: You should not hear smacking or clicking sounds.
LI: They should feed calmly and not move on and off the breast.
LI: Your baby finishes feeding and seems satisfied.
LI: You will feel comfortable during a feed and your nipples should not be sore.

P: Try to:
LI: let your baby attach gently - you do not need to push your baby onto your breast
LI: keep your baby's hands free - they use these to locate and massage your breast
P: To make sure they stay attached, try not to touch their lips or the back of their head during feeding.

CONTENT:
H1: Alcohol and breastfeeding
P: It’s important to avoid drinking alcohol until your baby is 1 month old.
P: In the first few weeks, you and your baby are getting used to breastfeeding. This is called establishing breastfeeding.
P: Alcohol in your breast milk can affect your baby’s sleep and development. Young babies have an immature liver and take longer to clear alcohol than an older baby or adult.
P: If your baby is premature or sick, ask your baby's doctor if it's OK to drink any alcohol.
P: Breastfeeding is usually established by the time your baby is 1 month old. If you chose to drink alcohol, you can avoid passing alcohol to your baby through your milk.
P: Regularly drinking alcohol can cause a decrease in your milk supply.
H2: Your baby's first month

H1: Domestic violence and abuse during pregnancy
P: Domestic violence is abuse of one person by another. Abuse can come from anybody. This includes someone close to you, such as a family member, a partner or a former partner.
H2: Types of abuse
P: Types of abuse include:
LI: physical
LI: emotional
LI: psychological
LI: sexual
LI: financial
LI: social
LI: online
P: Domestic violence is any behaviour that makes you feel:
LI: scared, nervous or anxious
LI: isolated from your family or friends
LI: your safety and wellbeing is threatened
P: Abuse can change or stop during pregnancy. But it can also get worse and become more dangerous. Being pregnant does not protect you from domestic violence.
P: It is very important to talk to somebody you trust. This could be a friend, a family member, a domestic violence support service or your GP.
H3: Emergency action required: Call the Gardaí on 112 or 999 if:
LI: you are in immediate danger
H2: Warning signs of an abusive relationship
P: An abuser can use many behaviours and actions to get power and control over their partner.
P: Abuse and violence in a relationship often get worse over time. Most victims in this situation will experience a range of abusive and controlling behaviours.
P: There are many signs of an abusive relationship with a current or ex-partner.
P: You may:
LI: feel afraid
LI: feel controlled
LI: feel isolated or alone
LI: get hurt or injured
LI: be forced to do things you do not want to do
LI: need to ask permission to do things - this is called coercive control
P: Abuse can change or stop during pregnancy, but it can also get worse. It can also become more dangerous and severe after the pregnancy and birth.
P: Coercive control - safeireland.ie
H2: Dangers during and after pregnancy
P: Experiencing any form of abuse during pregnancy can be very dangerous.
P: Pregnancy or just after having a baby is known as a high risk time for women in abusive relationships.
P: Violence can be especially severe. It can lead to death, pregnancy loss, injuries and mental health concerns.
P: Domestic violence means you are more likely to have:
LI: infections
LI: poor mental health
LI: a premature birth
LI: a low birth weight baby
LI: poor weight gain during pregnancy
LI: other serious pregnancy health complications

P: Miscarriage

TITLE: Pain relief in labour
CONTENT:
H1: Pain relief in labour
P: Labour can be painful and no method of pain relief is 100% effective.
P: Talk to your midwife during your pregnancy about labour pain relief options. They can tell you if your maternity unit, hospital or home birth midwife can accommodate your choice.
P: During labour you may need less or more pain relief than you had planned. Your midwife or obstetrician might suggest options to help with the birth.
H2: Self-help in labour
P: You're likely to feel more relaxed in labour and better placed to cope with the pain if you:
LI: prepare for it by learning about labour and going to antenatal classes
LI: practice relaxation and breathing techniques
LI: keep moving and try different positions using birthing balls and equipment - this can distract you from any discomfort you may be feeling
LI: bring a birth partner to support you during labour, but do not worry if you do not have a partner – your midwife will give you all the support you need
LI: go for a bath or shower - some hospitals may have birthing pools
H2: Gas and air (Entonox)
P: You can be given gas and air (Entonox) to help with labour pain. Entonox is half nitrous oxide and half oxygen.
P: You breathe in (inhale) the Entonox through a mouthpiece. You hold the mouthpiece over your mouth and breathe in when a contraction begins. You can use Entonox at any time during labour.
P: Using Entonox will not take the pain away completely but will reduce it. It also helps to focus your breathing. It takes 15 to 20 seconds to work. It works best if you use it at the start of a contraction and take long, deep breaths.
P: There are no harmful side-effects to you or your baby. But it can make you feel dizzy, light-headed or a little nauseous. If you find these symptoms are bothering you, stop using it and the effects wear off very quickly.
H2: Pethidine painkiller
P: Pethidine is a strong painkiller. It is a similar drug to morphine and a type of opioid. Pethidine is given as an injection into your leg or bottom. It may help lessen your pain, especially if used with other methods.
P: The injection can make you feel drowsy, dizzy or sick. It takes about 20 minutes to work and lasts 2 to 4 hours.
P: If given too close to birth, your baby may be a bit sleepy after birth.
P: Pethidine is not suitable for some people. For example, people with a history of epilepsy.
H2: Epidural
P: An epidural is a local anaesthetic given in your lower back. A drip will be put in a vein in your arm to give you some intravenous fluids beforehand. These keep your blood pressure stable.
H3: How an epidural is given
P: Before they begin, the anaesthetist will ask you to sit up in a slouched position or lie on your side.
LI: The anaesthetist cleans your skin and injects some local anaesthetic.
LI: They place a needle in your back.
LI: They pass a very thin tube through the needle into your back near the nerves.
LI: When the tube is in the right place, they remove the needle.
LI: The pain-relieving drugs are given through the tube.
P: It takes about 10 minutes to set up the epidural tube, and another 10 to 15 minutes for it to work.
P: A tube called a catheter will be inserted into your bladder to drain urine when the epidural is in place. It stays in place for up to 8 hours after the birth.
P: Your midwife will check your blood pressure regularly as sometimes it can fall.
H3: Epidural side-effects
P: The epidural has a minimal effect on your baby. It does not cause your baby to be sleepy.
P: You may need to remain in bed for the birth. Your baby will need to be monitored during labour.
P: Taking an epidural may make the labour longer and reduce your urge to push down. If you cannot feel your contractions, the midwife will tell you when to push and breathe out. Forceps or a ventouse (vacuum) may be needed to help deliver your baby.
P: The epidural may cause a severe headache, which can be treated.
P: It can also cause:
LI: a feeling of heaviness in the legs
LI: low blood pressure (the fluid in your drip should prevent this)
LI: pins and needles down one leg
P: Epidurals can cause soreness in the back for a day or 2. But they do not cause long-term back pain.
H2: TENS machine
P: The word 'TENS' stands for transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation. This involves passing a gentle electrical current through flat pads on your back. The small electric current stimulates the body to produce endorphins. Endorphins are natural painkillers.
H3: Using a TENS machine
P: Using a TENS machine works better if you start early in labour.
P: You, your birth partner or midwife tape the electrode pads to your back. These are connected to a small battery-operated stimulator. You can hold this and give yourself small doses of electric current.
P: Check if your maternity unit supplies TENS machines. If not, you may be able to rent one. Many large pharmacies offer these for sale or rent.
H2: Aromatherapy and alternative therapies
P: Aromatherapy oils can be used:
LI: in massage
LI: in the bath
LI: on a tissue
LI: by diffuser
P: These may increase relaxation and reduce pain. Speak with a qualified aromatherapist and check with your maternity unit or hospital before using aromatherapy.
P: Other options include hypnobirthing, yoga, acupuncture or reflexology. These may not be offered in your maternity hospital or unit but may be available privately.
P: Talk to your midwife if you are considering any alternative methods of pain relief. Many hospitals do not provide these services but may accommodate you.
